## How to use this file

Each story has routing tags so the prep-brief skill can match the right story to the right question
type. Common tags: `leadership`, `conflict`, `ambiguity`, `technical-depth`, `0-to-1`, `metrics`,
`influence-without-authority`, `failure`, `prioritization`, `cross-functional`.

---

## Story format — the arc every story should follow

Stronger than plain STAR: it foregrounds *tension* and *judgment*, so each story has a protagonist who
shows character, not a checklist of what happened.

1. **Hook** — one line that grabs attention and promises a payoff. "Let me tell you about the time I
   [did the surprising thing] and [got the outcome]." Make them want the rest.
2. **Stakes** — the situation and what was on the line, compressed. Why it mattered.
3. **Tension** — the conflict: the resistance, the against-the-grain fork, the moment the obvious path
   and the right path diverged. Name what made it hard and what others wanted or believed.
4. **Judgment** — the action *and why it took insight or nerve*. The non-obvious, harder, or riskier
   choice, and the reasoning that made it right. This is where courage and judgment show — the point.
5. **Impact** — the punchy, metric-backed result. One or two numbers that land.
6. **Lesson (bonus)** — the transferable insight; how it changed the way you think.

Deliver the hook first, let the tension build before the resolution, and never bury the judgment call in
a flat list of actions.

**Never fabricate — non-negotiable.** Every element must be true to what actually happened. Do not invent
tension, resistance, stakes, numbers, or detail to make a story stronger. If a stronger element is
missing, mark it as a prompt for the user to supply (e.g., *[add the real number]*), never guess. A
weaker true story beats a stronger invented one — invented detail collapses under interview probing.
